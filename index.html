Spirited Away (千と千尋の神隠し) is a 2001 Japanese animated fantasy film written and directed by Hayao Miyazaki and produced by Studio Ghibli. It became the highest-grossing film in Japanese history and won the Academy Award for Best Animated Feature.

The story follows Chihiro Ogino, a ten-year-old girl who enters the spirit world by accident. After her parents are transformed into pigs, she must work at a bathhouse run by the witch Yubaba to find a way to free herself and her family.

01
The Crossing

While moving to a new town, Chihiro and her family take a wrong turn and end up in what her father believes is an abandoned theme park. As night falls, her parents begin eating spirit food — and are transformed into pigs. Chihiro is suddenly alone in a world she doesn't understand.

02
The Bathhouse

With help from a mysterious boy named Haku, Chihiro enters the bathhouse — a grand establishment serving the gods and spirits of Japan. The witch Yubaba rules this world, taking workers' names to bind them to her service.

03
Becoming Sen

Yubaba takes Chihiro's name, renaming her Sen. Stripped of her identity, she throws herself into hard work, befriending Lin and discovering that Haku has a painful secret connected to her past.

04
No-Face

A silent spirit called No-Face — drawn to Chihiro's kindness — enters the bathhouse and grows monstrous in his loneliness. Only Sen can calm him. His story mirrors the film's meditation on greed and what it means to be truly seen.

05
The Sea Train

To save Haku, Sen must travel across a flooded landscape by train to visit Yubaba's twin sister Zeniba. A quiet, melancholy journey — a train crossing shallow water under a grey sky.

06
Remembering

Sen remembers Haku's true name — the spirit of the Kohaku River. Her memory breaks Yubaba's spell. Given one final test, she passes by trusting her instincts and crosses back with her parents restored.

Deeper Meaning

Themes & Symbolism

01
Identity & Memory

Yubaba steals names to control. Chihiro's journey is about holding onto who she is — identity is both fragile and unbreakable.

02
Work & Dignity

Chihiro earns her freedom through honest labor. Miyazaki presents work not as punishment but as something that gives life purpose.

03
Greed & Consumption

No-Face consuming everything mirrors the film's critique of unchecked materialism and Japan's economic bubble.

04
Nature & Spirits

Haku is a river spirit. The stink god is a polluted river. Miyazaki's ecological grief is embedded in every spirit.

05
Growing Up

Chihiro begins the film whining about moving. She ends it having outgrown everyone. Fear becomes courage.

06
Kindness as Power

Every character saved — Haku, No-Face, the river god — is saved by Chihiro's instinctive empathy, not magic or strength.
